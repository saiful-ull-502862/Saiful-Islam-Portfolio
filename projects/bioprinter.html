Compatible Bio-printing System for Surgery

2022 - 2023
4MLAB, UL Lafayette
Budget: $1,000
#3D Printing #CFD #Bioengineering #Tissue Engineering #Innovation

Project Overview

This innovative project successfully converted a standard FDM 3D printer into a functional bioprinter for PCL-based hydrogel printing under a strict $1,000 budget constraint. Through computational fluid dynamics (CFD) optimization of the extrusion system, we achieved 100% cell viability during direct-ink printing, making bioprinting technology accessible for academic laboratories with limited resources.

Innovation

Democratizing bioprinting technology through cost-effective conversion while maintaining clinical-grade cell viability through computational optimization of printing parameters.

Project Objectives

  • Convert standard FDM printer to bioprinter within $1,000 budget
  • Design and optimize extrusion system for hydrogel bioinks
  • Achieve >95% cell viability during printing process
  • Enable PCL-based scaffold fabrication for surgical applications
  • Validate system performance through experimental testing
  • Create reproducible protocol for other research laboratories

Methodology

Hardware Modifications

  • Base Platform: Commercial FDM 3D printer with motion control system
  • Extrusion System: Custom pneumatic dispensing system for hydrogels
  • Temperature Control: Heated print bed and nozzle for PCL processing
  • Sterile Environment: UV sterilization and HEPA filtration integration
  • Pressure Control: Precision pneumatic regulator for consistent extrusion

CFD Optimization

  • Nozzle Design: Computational modeling of flow through custom nozzle geometries
  • Shear Stress Analysis: Minimizing mechanical stress on cells during extrusion
  • Pressure Distribution: Optimizing print head geometry for uniform bioink flow
  • Parameter Sweep: Exploring extrusion pressure, nozzle diameter, and print speed
  • Software: ANSYS Fluent for CFD simulations

Experimental Validation

  • PCL/gelatin hydrogel formulation and rheological characterization
  • Cell encapsulation (fibroblasts, chondrocytes) in bioink
  • Live/dead assays for cell viability assessment
  • Scaffold printing and mechanical testing
  • Long-term cell culture studies on printed constructs

Key Achievements

Project Outcomes:

  • Successfully converted FDM printer to bioprinter for under $1,000
  • Achieved 100% cell viability during direct-ink extrusion
  • Optimized nozzle geometry reducing shear stress by 60%
  • Demonstrated successful PCL scaffold fabrication for cartilage repair
  • Maintained cell functionality and proliferation post-printing
  • Created open-source documentation for replication by other labs

Applications

  • Tissue Engineering: Fabrication of patient-specific tissue scaffolds
  • Surgical Planning: Creating anatomical models with integrated bioinks
  • Drug Testing: 3D tissue models for pharmaceutical screening
  • Wound Healing: Direct bioprinting of skin grafts
  • Cartilage Repair: PCL/cell scaffolds for joint resurfacing
  • Education: Hands-on bioprinting for bioengineering curriculum

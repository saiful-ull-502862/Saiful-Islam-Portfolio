Quantitative Image Analysis & FE Modeling of Cartilage Degradation

2024 - Present
4MLAB, UL Lafayette
Dr. Tanvir Faisal
#Image Analysis #FEA #Biomechanics #Mechanobiology #Osteoarthritis

Project Overview

Progressive collagen network degradation represents a critical pathomechanical feature of osteoarthritis (OA), disrupting cartilage load-bearing capacity and altering the mechanical microenvironment of chondrocytes. This study combines quantitative image analysis (IA) with computational biomechanics to map enzymatic collagen degradation patterns and predict their impact on cartilage tissue mechanics and chondrocyte deformation behavior.

Key Innovation

Integrated experimental-computational framework establishing quantitative relationships between tissue composition and cellular mechanics, enabling prediction of disease progression patterns and identification of biomechanical intervention targets.

Research Objectives

  • Map spatial heterogeneity of collagen degradation using quantitative image analysis
  • Characterize zone-specific biomechanical consequences of collagen loss
  • Establish quantitative relationship between matrix degradation severity and cellular mechanical strain
  • Predict impact of degradation on chondrocyte mechanobiological responses
  • Develop validated FE models incorporating key ECM constituents (collagen fibrils, PGs, PCM)

Methodology

Experimental Framework

  • Enzymatic Digestion: MMPs and ADAMTS treatment to simulate pathological degradation
  • Histology: Picrosirius Red staining and sectioning at 5 µm thickness
  • Quantification: Image analysis based on red intensity, validated by FTIR spectroscopy
  • Degradation Mapping: IA-derived degradation values: 25% (Superficial), 22% (Middle), 16% (Deep)

Computational Modeling

  • FE Model: Axisymmetric model incorporating type II/VI collagen, PGs, PCM, and chondrocytes
  • Validation: Validated against healthy cartilage properties
  • Simulation: Implemented graded collagen loss to investigate chondrocyte responses

Key Findings

Research Outcomes:

  • Depth-Dependent Degradation: Significantly higher degradation in Superficial Zone (24.62 ± 14.42%) vs Deep Zone (15.80 ± 10.51%)
  • Cellular Strain: Collagen degradation induced chondrocyte aspect ratio changes of 6.35% (SZ), 5.99% (MZ), and 3.77% (DZ)
  • Mechanobiology: Established quantitative link between matrix degradation and cellular mechanical strain
  • Pathology Insight: Confirmed collagen network integrity is critical for protecting cells from excessive strain

Significance & Clinical Relevance

This integrated approach addresses a critical gap in understanding how early-stage matrix degradation drives chondrocyte mechanical dysfunction. By linking tissue composition to cellular mechanics, the framework enables:

  • Prediction of disease progression patterns
  • Identification of biomechanical intervention targets
  • Development of structure-modifying therapies
  • Early diagnostic strategies for osteoarthritis

Future Directions

  • Integration of concurrent proteoglycan loss simulation
  • Experimental validation of predicted cellular deformations
  • Extension to patient-specific models for personalized medicine
  • Application to other connective tissues and pathologies

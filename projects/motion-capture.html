Project Overview

This research project investigates knee joint mechanics under military load carriage conditions (30-50% body weight) to predict osteoarthritis (OA) risk in veterans. Using advanced markerless motion capture technology combined with finite element analysis, we analyze how heavy loads affect knee biomechanics and long-term joint health.

Research Focus

Understanding the biomechanical mechanisms behind OA development in military personnel who regularly carry heavy loads during training and deployment. This work aims to develop predictive models for injury risk assessment and prevention strategies.

Research Objectives

  • Capture and analyze gait patterns under various military load conditions using markerless motion capture
  • Quantify knee joint loading patterns, contact stresses, and cartilage deformation
  • Develop FEA models integrating motion capture data for tissue-level stress analysis
  • Identify biomechanical risk factors for OA development in load-bearing populations
  • Propose evidence-based recommendations for load distribution and training modifications
  • Create predictive models for long-term joint health assessment in military personnel

Methodology

Experimental Protocol

  • Motion Capture: Markerless 3D motion capture system to record gait kinematics and kinetics
  • Loading Conditions: Progressive load testing (30%, 40%, 50% body weight) simulating military gear
  • Gait Analysis: Comprehensive evaluation of temporal-spatial parameters, joint angles, and ground reaction forces
  • Subject Pool: Healthy participants with varied anthropometric characteristics

Computational Analysis

  • Motion Data Processing: Python-based algorithms for kinematic data extraction and filtering
  • Inverse Dynamics: Calculation of joint moments and forces during gait cycle
  • FEA Integration: Application of motion-derived loading conditions to anatomically accurate knee models
  • Cartilage Stress Analysis: Prediction of contact pressures and tissue-level stresses
  • Statistical Analysis: Comparison across loading conditions and correlation with OA risk factors

Tools & Technologies

  • Markerless motion capture system (high-speed cameras and AI-based pose estimation)
  • Python (OpenCV, NumPy, SciPy) for motion data processing
  • MATLAB for biomechanical calculations and data visualization
  • ABAQUS for finite element simulations
  • Statistical analysis software for hypothesis testing

Key Findings & Progress

Research Outcomes:

  • Identified significant increases in peak knee joint loading (30-45%) under heavy load conditions
  • Documented altered gait patterns including reduced stride length and increased stance phase duration
  • Quantified cartilage contact stress increases of up to 60% in medial compartment under 50% body weight
  • Established correlation between load magnitude and biomechanical OA risk indicators
  • Developed markerless motion capture protocol validated against traditional marker-based systems
  • Created subject-specific FEA framework integrating real-time motion data

Clinical & Military Applications

This research has direct implications for:

  • Military Training: Evidence-based load limits and training protocols to minimize OA risk
  • Equipment Design: Guidelines for load distribution systems (backpacks, body armor)
  • Injury Prevention: Early identification of high-risk individuals for targeted interventions
  • Veteran Healthcare: Predictive models for OA screening and preventive care
  • Rehabilitation: Personalized exercise programs based on biomechanical analysis
  • Occupational Health: Applications to other load-bearing professions (firefighters, construction workers)

Future Directions

  • Longitudinal studies tracking OA development in military cohorts over 5-10 years
  • Integration of machine learning for real-time gait quality assessment
  • Development of wearable sensors for field deployment biomechanical monitoring
  • Investigation of interventions (strength training, gait retraining) to mitigate load effects
  • Expansion to full-body biomechanical analysis including spine and lower extremities

Collaborations & Funding

This project is conducted in collaboration with military research institutions and biomechanics laboratories, with the goal of improving service member health and readiness.

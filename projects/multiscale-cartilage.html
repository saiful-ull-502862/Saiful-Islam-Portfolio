Multi-scale Modeling of Articular Cartilage

August 2021 - Present
4MLAB, UL Lafayette
Dr. Tanvir Faisal
#FEA #Biomechanics #ABAQUS #Python #Multi-scale Modeling

Project Overview

This research project focuses on developing advanced multi-scale finite element models to compare the mechanical behavior of healthy and degenerative articular cartilage. The work is critical for understanding osteoarthritis (OA) progression and developing better diagnostic and treatment strategies.

By integrating micro-scale fibril architecture with macro-scale tissue behavior through computational homogenization techniques, this project bridges the gap between molecular-level cartilage structure and whole-joint mechanics.

Research Objectives

  • Develop validated micro-FE models to simulate cartilage mechanics at the microscale level
  • Create a 3D multi-scale model using homogenization methods to link microstructure to bulk behavior
  • Compare mechanical responses between healthy and degenerative cartilage states
  • Predict osteoarthritis progression through computational simulations
  • Optimize model parameters to match experimental data from biomechanical testing

Methodology

Computational Framework

The modeling approach employs a multi-structural fibril-reinforced poro-hyperelastic (MSFPH) finite element framework implemented in ABAQUS. Key components include:

  • Microstructure Modeling: Representative Volume Elements (RVEs) capturing zone-specific collagen fibril orientation and density
  • Material Characterization: Poro-hyperelastic constitutive models accounting for fluid-solid interactions and large deformations
  • Homogenization: Mathematical techniques to derive effective properties from microstructural simulations
  • Validation: Comparison with experimental indentation and compression test data
  • Parametric Studies: Python-based automation for exploring degradation scenarios

Computational Tools

  • ABAQUS/Standard and Explicit for finite element simulations
  • Python scripting for model automation and post-processing
  • MATLAB for data analysis and visualization
  • SolidWorks for geometric modeling

Key Findings

Research Outcomes:

  • Successfully validated micro-FE models against experimental nanoindentation data with <95% error
  • Demonstrated zone-specific mechanical behavior correlates with collagen fibril architecture
  • Identified critical degradation thresholds where cartilage mechanics significantly deteriorate
  • Developed predictive framework for OA progression based on microstructural changes
  • Established computational homogenization workflow reducing simulation time by 80%

Impact & Future Directions

This research has significant implications for:

  • Clinical Applications: Early detection of OA through computational biomarkers
  • Treatment Planning: Patient-specific simulations for optimizing interventions
  • Tissue Engineering: Design criteria for cartilage scaffolds and regenerative therapies
  • Drug Development: In silico testing platform for disease-modifying OA drugs
